To the girl,
to the woman,
to my love

I.

You asked for a poem about rain.
I wrote about the way your sleeve
brushed mine on the ferry.
That was twenty years ago.
Still, I haven't finished.

The rain keeps falling,
on the harbour, on the roofs,
on the cars that carry strangers
to places we'll never go.

I think of you whenever
the forecast says "persistent rain."
Such a persistent word, "persistent."
Like the way I persist in remembering
the exact shade of your wet coat,
the way you shook your hair
like a dog, laughing,
and I pretended to mind.

III.

The neighbour's dog digs under the fence again.
You'd laugh, say he's looking for the bones we buried.
We never buried any bones.
Maybe he smells all the things we didn't say.

All those unsaid things —
they must be somewhere.
Under the soil, in the roots,
working their way up through stems.

The dog digs furiously,
throws dirt behind him.
He's looking for the words
we swallowed, the apologies
we left to rot, the I-love-yous
we thought didn't need saying.

He hasn't found them yet.
Neither have I.
But every spring, something
blooms where he digs —
small white flowers,
nameless, persistent.

V.

The recipe is torn — half a teaspoon?
I add a pinch of memory, a guess.
The cake still rises,
but it tastes like the year you left.
Sweet, but with a sinkhole in the middle.

I've made it every year since.
2015: too much salt, like tears.
2016: burnt on the bottom,
charcoal, like the news that year.
2017: perfect outside, raw inside —
I gave it to the birds.

This year, I follow the ghost
of your handwriting, the faded
'add a little love' in the margin.
I add love. I add butter.
I cream them together
until they're light, until they're one.

The cake rises evenly.
I eat a slice. It's good.
The sinkhole is smaller this year.
Or maybe I've just learned
to eat around it.

VII.

Grandmother's thimble fits my pinky.
It's silver, worn thin as paper.
I think of her fingers, pushing needles
through linen, through hours,
through two wars. I sew a button.
The thimble is warm.

She survived the siege by sewing,
hemming officers' trousers for bread,
darning socks for soup.
The thimble protected her,
a small silver helmet
for the fingertip that pushed
through thread, through time.

Now it protects me
from nothing more than a needle's prick.
But when I wear it, I feel her —
the steady pulse of her hand,
the rhythm of stitch after stitch,
the way she'd pause to look out the window
at whatever war was passing.

I sew a button. It holds.
The thimble gleams on my pinky,
a small inheritance of perseverance,
passed down through blood, through thread,
through the simple, radical act
of mending what is torn.

IX.

The key doesn't fit any lock in the house.
I keep it because your fingers touched it.
Some afternoons I hold it, walk from room to room,
trying doors that have always been open.

It doesn't fit the front door,
the back door, the bedroom,
the small lock on the cabinet
where you kept your letters.

I've tried the shed, the garage,
the rusty lock on the garden gate.
Nothing.

Maybe it fits a lock that no longer exists —
a suitcase lost on a train in 1973,
a diary you kept when you were twelve,
a box buried in the yard of a house
that was torn down to build a road.

Or maybe it doesn't fit anything.
Maybe it's just a key,
beautiful in its uselessness,
warm from your fingers,
warm now from mine.

I put it back in the drawer.
It lies among the paperclips,
the spare batteries, the stamps,
waiting for a lock
that will never appear.
Waiting, like me,
for a click that won't come.
